Intra-Urban Air Temperature Variability in Swiss Cities:
Machine Learning Approaches for Enhancing Low-Coast Device Monitoring Networks

Learning Outcome

INTRODUCTION Anthropogenic climate change is expected to increase the frequency, intensity, and du- ration of heat waves (IPCC Report, 2021). The boreal summer of 2023 was the fifth warmest on record, 0.83 ◦C higher than the 1991-2020 average of 19.63 ◦C (Copernicus Climate Change Service, 2023). Urban areas are increasingly experiencing the impacts of heat extremes (Lorenz et al., 2019), particularly through the phenomenon known as the Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect (Oke, 2006). This effect causes urban areas to become significantly warmer than their rural surroundings due to human activities, dense infras- tructure, and land-use changes. The warming effect is further exacerbated during the night, as urban areas retain heat more effectively than rural areas, leading to increased human heat stress and causing serious risks to public health (Cheval et al., 2024), includ- ing a higher risk of mortality (Huang et al., 2023).

People living in urban areas are highly affected by the UHI effect through thermal stress (Burger et al., 2021; Wicki et al., 2018). Given that more than 75 % of the Central Eu- ropean population resides in urban areas, this increasing trend poses one of the major weather-related threats to people in these environments (Wicki et al., 2018). To miti- gate threats to public health, detailed knowledge of temperature distribution is necessary (Zumwald et al., 2021). One of the most effective methods for monitoring urban heat stress is through detailed heat-maps, which visualize temperature variations across a city (Zumwald et al., 2021; Völkel and Shandas, 2021). These maps are essential for effective urban planning and public health interventions; however, their accuracy often depends on costly automated weather stations (AWS) to capture temperature data (Gubler et al., 2021) and on the models used.

To capture small-scale temperature changes in a city, a climatically complex area, high spatial resolution measurement networks are needed. Gubler et al. (2021) pioneered this approach by developing low-cost devices (LCDs) to build an extensive temperature net- work in the city of Bern, Switzerland. The LCDs consist of a temperature logger and a custom-made, naturally ventilated radiation shield. In 2023, Gabriel Erisman, a Mas- ter’s student at the Oeschger Center for Climate Change Research, University of Bern, Switzerland, set up a monitoring network in the city of Biel, Switzerlan as part of his thesis, similar to the one in Bern. The network in Biel consists of 39 LCDs covering all occurring local climate zones (LCZs) according to Oke and Stewart (2012). The temporal resolution of the network is set at 10-minute intervals, while the spatial resolution varies significantly depending on the location of the LCDs (approx. 50 m to 2000 m). The data in the LCDs must be read out manually every month.

Weather information is important for many aspects from everyday life to spatial planning and climate change adaptation. Reconstructions of past weather extend our knowledge by covering a larger time window, which is particularly important for extremes. On this site you can learn step-by-step how historical manuscript data can eventually lead to complex climate information such as hazard maps.
