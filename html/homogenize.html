Homogenize

Even after QC, meteorological series may reflect non-climatic influences. For instance, a replaced thermometer or the relocation of a weather screen may have led to an artificial step change in temperature. Such effects are called inhomogeneities, and the process of removing them is called homogenization. Statistically, step inhomogeneities are called break points. To detect break points, climatologists compare a candidate series with one or several reference series, which are highly correlated with the candidate series, such as series from neighbouring locations. The reference series is supposed to be homogeneous. By analysing only the differences, the real weather variability (common to both series) is suppressed, thus better exposing artificial aspects.

A simple way to find breaks in meteorological time series is the Craddock test. The basic principle is to accumulate the differences (for precipitation or wind ratios are used instead of differences). In a homogenous section of the series, the accumulated differences build a straight line. Breakpoints are recognizable subjectively as breaks or bends in the accumulated differences.
